At AI Data hive Manchester, Arushi Singh and Phil Whittaker's talks both agreed that LLMs are probabilistic. What does this mean? It means that they find the highest probability response from patterns and how they have been trained, they aren't (usually) doing their own calculations or thinking.

You can prompt for determination though:
* <span class="bg-emerald-500/10 text-emerald-400 px-1.5 py-0.5 rounded border border-emerald-500/20 font-mono text-[0.9em] whitespace-nowrap">Strict constraints</span> - answers yes/no - this can reduce the risks of rambling.
* <span class="bg-emerald-500/10 text-emerald-400 px-1.5 py-0.5 rounded border border-emerald-500/20 font-mono text-[0.9em] whitespace-nowrap">Step-by-step logic</span> - This creates a chain of thought, forcing the AI to show its work which encourages rigid logical track to be followed.

<span class="bg-emerald-500/10 text-emerald-400 px-1.5 py-0.5 rounded border border-emerald-500/20 font-mono text-[0.9em] whitespace-nowrap">Synonyms are not interchangeable for LLMs!!!</span> (even if you tell them they are) - This slide on Arushi Singh's talk started me thinking. 
If you use a particular word with an LLM it will affect the response you get, and not just because they like patterns, but as I confirmed with her after the talk its based on the data they are trained on.

LLMs are probabilistic, Synonyms are not interchangeable, which is the first reason why I am exceptional at using LLMs.

## Linguistic Fluidity as a technical skill

> "The "Synonym" Argument, or Semantic Density vs Vector space, is basically the argument of how meaning of words is measured."

I lived in Malta for 9 years, and the majority of people I worked with in person and online spoke English as a 2nd or 3rd language. When talking to someone, and you pay attention to them, their body language and responses, you can tell when you aren't being understood, this was almost always because the way they were taught English was different in Sweden, Germany, France, Spain, Italy, and they might have learnt a particular English word but not all 17 words, so quickly using a different word, expression or example allowed comprehension and to continue. 

I developed my ability to <span class="bg-emerald-500/10 text-emerald-400 px-1.5 py-0.5 rounded border border-emerald-500/20 font-mono text-[0.9em] whitespace-nowrap">translate English to English</span>, often in a call with someone Spanish, Swedish and Maltese this would happen because someone would say a phrase or expression completely correctly but wouldn't be understood. My interjections were often appreciated by both sides of the divide, however this just seemed normal to me. This is one of the pillars of my Linguistic fluidity as a technical skill, my ability and experience in swapping a synonym in real time to achieve understanding.

## Iterative development

<span class="bg-emerald-500/10 text-emerald-400 px-1.5 py-0.5 rounded border border-emerald-500/20 font-mono text-[0.9em] whitespace-nowrap">Socratic questioning</span> is how I teach people, put simply is I ask people leading questions, sometimes open questions and sometimes closed questions, but I have developed this method through University tutoring, training people at HSBC, onboarding new compliance staff or direct reports, when answering questions when I deliver training to groups, and even when talking to my partner or friends.

It means naturally I want to work iteratively, which follows the excellent ways of building or working with LLMs:
* <span class="bg-emerald-500/10 text-emerald-400 px-1.5 py-0.5 rounded border border-emerald-500/20 font-mono text-[0.9em] whitespace-nowrap">Chain of thoughts</span> when wishing to attempt determinism.
* <span class="bg-emerald-500/10 text-emerald-400 px-1.5 py-0.5 rounded border border-emerald-500/20 font-mono text-[0.9em] whitespace-nowrap">Tree of thoughts (ToT)</span> where multiple steps are taken towards a solution to a complex problem. 

This method of use implements <span class="bg-emerald-500/10 text-emerald-400 px-1.5 py-0.5 rounded border border-emerald-500/20 font-mono text-[0.9em] whitespace-nowrap">output grounding</span>, by not relying on a significant initial prompt and allowing control over each smaller iterative step, and allows for control over the intention gap, as divergence can be spotted and shut down or corrected for.

This allows me to prevent <span class="bg-emerald-500/10 text-emerald-400 px-1.5 py-0.5 rounded border border-emerald-500/20 font-mono text-[0.9em] whitespace-nowrap">context flooding</span>, my prompts do not need to be a huge chunk of text which will take up all of my agents' recourse. I'm able to navigate both dynamic context management and active context management, without having to develop skills, allowing more agile workflows. My experience working for large companies and interesting projects from HSBC, Flutter, Betway, Allwyn (4th national lottery) and now a government regulator mean I know when to add guard rails to a prompt and say to not do something, or to avoid something, or to note it and not develop it.

## "Emotional Stimuli" (or Emotion prompting)

The same language use and probabilistic nature has been revealed by studies that phrases such as *"This is vital for my career"* or *"You'd better be sure about this"* are able to improve an LLM's performance by up to 115% on certain tasks. 

LLMs aren't "feeling" the pressure, but as they are probabilistic and so the language and this type of situation receives a response based on similar content it was trained on. So this type of language creates <span class="bg-emerald-500/10 text-emerald-400 px-1.5 py-0.5 rounded border border-emerald-500/20 font-mono text-[0.9em] whitespace-nowrap">domain activation</span> due to the high-stakes language, the training documents the response is drawn from (legal documents, emergency procedures, "this is important" emails) which have normally been created with much higher precision and care than Reddit or 4chan.

The math of it looks something like this:

$$ P(\text{Precision} \mid \text{Urgency}) > P(\text{Precision} \mid \text{Generic Instruction}) $$
